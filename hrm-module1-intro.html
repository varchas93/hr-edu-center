Definition and Evolution of HRM

Definition

HRM is the strategic approach to managing an organization's workforce to maximize performance, ensure compliance, and foster a positive work environment.

Evolution of HRM

Personnel Management

Early 20th Century

Focused on administrative tasks (payroll, hiring, firing).

Example: Factory supervisors handling worker attendance manually.

Human Relations

1930s-1950s

Emphasized employee satisfaction and motivation.

Example: Hawthorne Studies on productivity.

Strategic HRM

1980s-Present

HR aligns with business goals and analytics.

Example: Google's People Analytics.

Key Functions of HR

Administrative HR Functions

Transactional tasks: Payroll, record-keeping, compliance.

Example: An HR assistant processing employee tax forms.

Strategic HR Functions

  • Talent Acquisition & Retention: Hiring top talent, reducing turnover.
  • Performance Management: Continuous feedback (e.g., Adobe's "Check-In" system).
  • Learning & Development: Upskilling employees (e.g., Amazon's $700M reskilling program).

Comparison

Administrative HR Strategic HR
Reactive (fixing issues) Proactive (planning ahead)
Short-term focus Long-term business impact
Paperwork & compliance Leadership & culture shaping

HR Ethics and Professional Standards

Key HR Ethics Principles

  • Confidentiality: Protecting employee data (e.g., GDPR compliance).
  • Fairness: Avoiding bias in hiring (e.g., blind recruitment at Deloitte).
  • Integrity: No favoritism in promotions.

Professional Bodies & Certifications

SHRM

Society for HR Management

Offers SHRM-CP/SHRM-SCP certifications

CIPD

Chartered Institute of Personnel & Development

UK-based HR standards

HRCI

HR Certification Institute

Offers PHR, SPHR certifications

Ethical Dilemma Example

Scenario: An HR manager discovers a top-performing employee lied on their resume.

Ethical Action: Follow company policy (investigate, possibly terminate) while ensuring fairness.

HR Compliance and Legal Framework

Key Employment Laws

Law Purpose Example Violation
FLSA Minimum wage, overtime Misclassifying employees as exempt
Title VII Prevents discrimination Rejecting a candidate based on race
FMLA Unpaid leave for emergencies Denying leave to an employee

Global Labor Standards

  • ILO: International Labour Organization standards (no child labor)
  • Example: Nike's sweatshop reforms in the 1990s

Case Study: TechNova Hiring Dilemma

Background

TechNova Inc. needs to hire a Chief Data Officer (CDO) within two weeks. Two candidates emerge:

Alex

  • 10+ years at Google/IBM
  • Strong technical skills
  • Bullying rumors (unverified)

Jordan

  • SHRM-certified leader
  • Excellent culture fit
  • Less AI experience

Stakeholder Views

CEO

"We need Alex—his big-name experience will impress clients!"

Team

"We prefer Jordan for better culture fit"

Legal

"No formal complaints against Alex"

Discussion Questions
